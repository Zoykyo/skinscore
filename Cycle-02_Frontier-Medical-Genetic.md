# Cycle 2 — Frontier Medical & Genetic

This cycle moves past the drugstore aisle into the dermatologist's office and the biotech pipeline: prescription retinoids, injectables, energy devices, growth factors and peptides, and the genuinely experimental frontier of senolytics, exosomes, polynucleotides, hormonal therapy and at-home genetics. These interventions attack the same Cycle 1 aging **levers** more aggressively than cosmetics can — tretinoin directly blocks the **oxidative→MMP photoaging** cascade and rebuilds collagen; energy devices and microneedling force **neocollagenesis** to counter **barrier/dermal decline**; topical/systemic estrogen replaces the **hormonal decline** that collapses collagen at menopause; and rapamycin plus senolytics are the first credible attempts to drug **cellular senescence, SASP and inflammaging** in human skin. The honest theme of this cycle: the evidence is *bimodal*. A handful of these (tretinoin, botulinum toxin, lasers) are among the best-proven interventions in all of dermatology. Much of the rest — exosomes, "salmon DNA," stem-cell serums, genetic skincare tests — is being sold years ahead of the RCT data, at clinic prices, and some of it is frankly hype. This is educational synthesis, **not medical advice**; every prescription drug, injectable and device below carries real risk and requires a **licensed dermatologist or physician**.

## Prescription topical tretinoin / retinoids — the medical gold standard

Tretinoin (all-*trans* retinoic acid) is the single most rigorously validated anti-aging topical in existence and the benchmark every cosmetic "retinol" is measured against. A [systematic review of RCTs (PMC9112391)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9112391/) identified eight randomized vehicle-controlled trials totalling ~1,361 patients, with tretinoin monotherapy significantly improving wrinkling, mottled hyperpigmentation, sallowness and lentigines. A [2025 network meta-analysis of 22 RCTs (3,801 participants)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-12597-0) ranked tretinoin among the most effective topicals for fine wrinkles (odds ratio ≈ 6.87 vs vehicle). Mechanistically it binds nuclear retinoic-acid receptors to **induce new collagen (types I/III), prevent collagen loss, and block UV induction of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)** — i.e., it hits the oxidative→MMP lever at the transcriptional level, per the [updated MDPI clinical review](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-0383/14/22/7958). Effect sizes are modest-but-real and take 3–6 months; the limiting factor is tolerability (retinoid dermatitis), which drives poor adherence. Prescription-only in many countries, teratogenic (avoid in pregnancy), photosensitizing — **consult a dermatologist/physician.**
**Scores — Ev 5, Imp 4, Saf 4, Eff 3, Cost 4 → SkinScore 83 (Core).**

## Topical peptides — GHK-Cu (copper peptide) and Matrixyl

Peptides are the most over-marketed "active" category, but two have real signal. **GHK-Cu** (copper tripeptide) modulates multiple regeneration pathways — it upregulates collagen/elastin and antioxidant genes and appears to nudge senescent-fibroblast behavior, reviewed mechanistically in [PMC4508379](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4508379/). Human data are thinner than the marketing implies: small RCTs report ~25–30% improvements in wrinkles/elasticity over 12 weeks, but much of the "evidence trail" is vendor-published and heterogeneous. **Matrixyl** (palmitoyl pentapeptide-4 / Matrixyl 3000) is a collagen-signal-mimicking peptide; a [2017 MDPI review of topical peptides](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9284/4/2/16) and a [12-week placebo-controlled study of pal-KTTKS](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jocd.15849)-class serums show modest (~10–30% vs baseline) wrinkle reduction — but the trials are overwhelmingly industry-sponsored cosmetic studies, not independent RCTs. Both are very safe and trivial to apply, which props up their scores; the honest read is "plausible marginal benefit, weak independent evidence." GHK-Cu edges Matrixyl on breadth of mechanistic support.
**GHK-Cu — Ev 3, Imp 3, Saf 5, Eff 4, Cost 3 → SkinScore 71 (Promising).**
**Matrixyl — Ev 3, Imp 2, Saf 5, Eff 4, Cost 3 → SkinScore 66 (Promising).**

## Topical growth factors (EGF)

Epidermal growth factor drives keratinocyte proliferation and fibroblast collagen deposition. A [2021 systematic review (PMC8423211)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8423211/) found conditioned-media/EGF serums over 8–12 weeks produced statistically significant reductions in wrinkle depth and gains in dermal thickness, and a [split-face RCT of micro-spicule EGF](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5383744/) improved periocular wrinkles. **The fatal caveat is delivery:** EGF is a ~6 kDa protein that penetrates intact stratum corneum poorly, so topical efficacy is limited and the strongest effects in the literature come from *intradermal* delivery or microneedle-assisted routes. As a stand-alone serum it is a weak-evidence, low-impact play; as a microneedling adjunct it is more defensible.
**Topical EGF — Ev 2, Imp 2, Saf 4, Eff 4, Cost 2 → SkinScore 54 (Experimental, top of band).**

## Exosome therapy (topical & injected)

Exosomes — nanovesicles carrying growth factors, miRNA and lipids, usually from mesenchymal or stem cells — are the hottest aesthetic-med buzzword of 2025–26, and the evidence is early. A [2026 systematic review & meta-analysis of human trials in *Aesthetic Surgery Journal*](https://academic.oup.com/asj/article/46/Supplement_1/S13/8512052) and a [Dermatology Practical & Conceptual systematic review](https://www.dpcj.org/index.php/dpc/article/view/6462) pool small studies (often ~8 studies, ~171 patients) showing improved hydration, elasticity, pigmentation and pore/texture when exosomes are combined with **microneedling** — but samples are tiny, protocols non-standardized, and almost all data are combination (not exosome-alone) designs. The decisive problem is regulatory and safety: **the FDA has approved NO exosome product for cosmetic or therapeutic use** and has issued warnings about unregulated products and infection risk ([Salisbury PS 2025 summary](https://salisburyps.com/exosome-serums-in-medical-skincare-clinical-evidence-fda-status-and-what-patients-need-to-know-in-2025/)). Genuinely promising biology, genuinely premature as a paid therapy. Injected/unregulated preparations carry real risk — **consult a physician.**
**Exosomes — Ev 2, Imp 3, Saf 2, Eff 2, Cost 1 → SkinScore 43 (Experimental).**

## Platelet-rich plasma (PRP, "vampire facial")

Autologous PRP concentrates the patient's own platelet growth factors, injected or microneedled into the face to trigger collagen synthesis. A [systematic appraisal of the clinical evidence (PMC8606573)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8606573/) found essentially all studies report improvement in wrinkles, texture and dermal density — including a few randomized split-face trials — **but** with wildly inconsistent preparation protocols and outcome measures, precluding firm conclusions. Safety is favorable (autologous, mostly transient bruising/erythema), though the "vampire facial" brand earned an FDA cautionary spotlight after HIV transmissions at an unlicensed spa using improper technique. Real but modest and poorly standardized effect; a reasonable clinic option with realistic expectations. In-office procedure — **use a licensed provider.**
**PRP — Ev 3, Imp 3, Saf 4, Eff 2, Cost 2 → SkinScore 59 (Promising).**

## Microneedling (± radiofrequency)

Microneedling creates controlled micro-wounds that release growth factors and drive **neocollagenesis and neoelastogenesis** — directly countering dermal/barrier decline. A [randomized controlled trial in JCAD](https://jcadonline.com/microneedling-topical-growth-factors-rejuvenation/) confirmed improvements in texture, wrinkles and hydration across four monthly sessions, enhanced by adding topical growth factors. **Radiofrequency microneedling** (RF delivered through the needles adds controlled dermal thermal injury) has stronger recent RCT support for wrinkle and laxity improvement — see [Frontiers in Medicine 2025 imaging-validated study](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2025.1710949/full) and [split-face RF-microneedling RCTs (PMC12452053)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12452053/). Plain microneedling is cheap, low-risk and even available at home (with hygiene caveats and lower efficacy); RF versions hit harder but are clinic-only, pricier, and carry burn/scarring risk in poor hands. Both are solidly "Promising."
**Microneedling — Ev 4, Imp 3, Saf 4, Eff 3, Cost 3 → SkinScore 70 (Promising).**
**RF microneedling — Ev 4, Imp 4, Saf 4, Eff 2, Cost 2 → SkinScore 70 (Promising).**

## Ablative & non-ablative fractional lasers (CO2, Fraxel) and IPL

Energy-based resurfacing is among the best-evidenced procedural anti-aging. **Ablative fractional CO2** (10,600 nm) vaporizes microcolumns of tissue to trigger aggressive epidermal regeneration and dermal collagen remodeling — the most dramatic wrinkle/texture improvements available short of surgery, at the cost of days-to-weeks of downtime and real risks (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation, scarring, infection). **Non-ablative fractional (Fraxel, 1550/1927 nm)** delivers milder but well-tolerated improvement; multiple RCTs (e.g., [investigator-blinded split-face Fraxel trial, PMC12104010](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12104010/); [non-ablative 1927-nm periorbital trial, PMC12178097](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12178097/)) show significant pigment and wrinkle gains with minimal downtime. **IPL** (broadband, not a true laser) excels at pigment/vascular photodamage and stimulates neocollagenesis: Bitter's foundational work showed serial IPL improved wrinkling, dyspigmentation and pore size in >90% of patients, and — strikingly — the [Chang et al. Stanford pilot](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22931923/) found broadband light **shifted the gene-expression pattern of aged skin toward a younger profile**, a rare demonstration of hitting the genomic/epigenetic lever. Because ablative CO2's evidence and impact are highest but safety is lowest, and IPL is safer but less transformative, I score fractional lasers as one blended "Promising" entry and IPL separately. All are clinician-operated — **consult a dermatologist.**
**Fractional lasers (CO2/Fraxel) — Ev 5, Imp 4, Saf 3, Eff 2, Cost 2 → SkinScore 72 (Promising).**
**IPL — Ev 4, Imp 3, Saf 4, Eff 3, Cost 2 → SkinScore 68 (Promising).**

## Injectables — botulinum toxin and hyaluronic acid fillers

**Botulinum toxin** (Botox/onabotulinumtoxinA) is, alongside tretinoin, the most evidence-backed anti-aging intervention in this cycle. A [Cochrane review (CD011301)](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD011301.pub2/full) and a [systematic review/meta-analysis of intradermal BoNT-A on skin quality (PMC11343530)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11343530/) confirm large, reliable reductions in dynamic (expression) wrinkles — glabella, forehead, crow's feet. It doesn't address the biology of aging, only the muscular contribution to wrinkles, but the effect is visible and reproducible; downsides are the 3–4-month duration (adherence/cost drag) and rare ptosis/asymmetry. **Hyaluronic acid fillers** restore volume loss and are strongly evidenced for nasolabial folds and structural rejuvenation; combination BoNT + HA data ([split-face RCTs, PMC12355333](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12355333/)) show additive, longer-lasting results. Fillers carry a lower safety score due to rare but serious vascular-occlusion/blindness risk if misinjected. Both are injectables requiring a **skilled licensed injector — consult a physician.**
**Botulinum toxin — Ev 5, Imp 4, Saf 4, Eff 3, Cost 2 → SkinScore 79 (Core).**
**HA fillers — Ev 5, Imp 4, Saf 3, Eff 3, Cost 2 → SkinScore 75 (Core, floor of band).**

## Senolytics (dasatinib + quercetin) and senomorphics (topical rapamycin)

This is the frontier that targets the **senescence/SASP** lever most directly. **Dasatinib + quercetin (D+Q)** is the flagship senolytic combo: an [open-label pilot in diabetic kidney disease (PMC6796530)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6796530/) showed just 3 days of oral D+Q reduced senescent-cell burden in adipose *and skin* (fewer p16^INK4A^+/p21+ cells) plus lower circulating SASP factors. That is a real human senolysis signal — but it is *systemic*, tiny, not a dermatology endpoint, and dasatinib is a serious chemotherapy drug with meaningful toxicity; **oral senolytics for skin are not an established or safe consumer intervention.** **Topical rapamycin** is the more skin-specific and encouraging story: the [Chung et al. 2019 exploratory RCT (PMC6925069)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6925069/) applied topical rapamycin to the hands of adults >40 and found significant reductions in **p16^INK4A^** (P=0.008) and increases in **collagen VII** (P=0.0077) with improved clinical/histological appearance — the first RCT-level evidence that an mTOR inhibitor can shift senescence markers in human skin. It's a small, single trial (high dropout), so evidence is mid-tier, but the mechanism is exactly on-target for inflammaging/senescence. Compounded/off-label — **consult a physician.**
**Senolytics (D+Q) — Ev 2, Imp 3, Saf 2, Eff 3, Cost 3 → SkinScore 50 (Experimental).**
**Topical rapamycin — Ev 3, Imp 3, Saf 3, Eff 3, Cost 3 → SkinScore 60 (Promising).**

## Polynucleotide / "salmon DNA" injectables (PDRN / PN, e.g. Rejuran)

Polynucleotides (PN) and polydeoxyribonucleotides (PDRN), derived from purified salmon/trout DNA, are injected to activate adenosine A2A receptors, stimulating angiogenesis, fibroblast activity and anti-inflammatory signaling; a [2025 model showed PDRN raised SIRT1 and suppressed p53/p16](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/15/19/10437), touching the senescence and oxidative levers. But aesthetic efficacy data are mixed: the largest controlled trial (Pak 2014, Phase III split-face, n=72) found **no significant difference vs hyaluronic acid**, and a [2025 systematic review in *J Cosmet Dermatol* (Lampridou)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jocd.16721) reports "varying degrees of efficacy," with some studies showing limited benefit. The **strongest** PDRN evidence is actually in **wound healing / post-procedure recovery** (faster re-epithelialization after CO2 laser, ulcers), not de-novo rejuvenation. Popular and reasonably safe (mild bruising/pain), but the rejuvenation claim outpaces the RCT data. Injectable — **consult a physician.**
**Polynucleotides — Ev 3, Imp 3, Saf 4, Eff 2, Cost 2 → SkinScore 59 (Promising).**

## Topical / systemic estrogen for skin

Menopausal estrogen loss is a major, often-ignored driver of skin aging — women lose ~30% of dermal collagen in the first ~5 years post-menopause. Estrogen therapy targets that **hormonal-decline** lever directly. A [JAAD Reviews synthesis on topical estrogen for aging skin](https://www.jaadreviews.org/article/S2950-1989(25)00103-5/fulltext) reports topical estradiol (~0.01%) consistently stimulates collagen across trials (histology, hydroxyproline, procollagen), and a pilot in women already on systemic HRT showed a further significant facial-collagen increase after 16 weeks (P<.001) — evidence of a *local* effect beyond systemic replacement, acting largely via cutaneous ER-β with minimal systemic absorption. **Systemic HRT** improves skin collagen/hydration too but with variable results and, critically, whole-body risk/benefit tradeoffs that must be decided for menopausal symptoms and overall health — **not** prescribed for wrinkles alone ([2025 narrative review, PMC12374573](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12374573/)). Prescription hormone therapy with real contraindications — **consult a physician/dermatologist.**
**Topical estrogen — Ev 3, Imp 3, Saf 3, Eff 4, Cost 3 → SkinScore 63 (Promising).**

## Nutrigenomics & at-home genetic tests for skincare

Direct-to-consumer "DNA skincare" tests (CircleDNA, SkinDNA, etc.) genotype a handful of SNPs (collagen, antioxidant, MMP, inflammation genes) and sell personalized routines/supplements. The scientific verdict is harsh: this is a **largely unregulated market with unsupported claims**, and the [foundational nutrigenomics review (PMC2989812)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2989812/) plus [proposed validity guidelines (PMC5732517)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5732517/) note that common variants explain only tiny fractions of any skin phenotype, and commercial tests give "simple answers to a complex question." The resulting recommendations almost always collapse to generic, evidence-based advice (wear sunscreen, use retinoids/antioxidants) that applies regardless of genotype. Essentially no evidence that acting on these tests improves skin outcomes; the main risk is wasted money and false precision. Low-harm, low-value.
**Nutrigenomic tests — Ev 1, Imp 1, Saf 4, Eff 3, Cost 2 → SkinScore 40 (Experimental, floor of band).**

## Stem-cell-based therapies

MSC (mesenchymal stem cell)-based rejuvenation — autologous adipose-derived stem cell (ADSC) injections, or "stem-cell" conditioned-media/secretome serums — is biologically rich but clinically immature. A [2025 Frontiers in Aging review](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/aging/articles/10.3389/fragi.2025.1638168/full) cites ADSC injections producing ~33–40% improvement in wrinkles/nasolabial folds in small series, and [ADSC-secretome reviews (PMC12610433)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12610433/) argue the *secretome/paracrine* signaling (not the cells themselves) drives repair — pointing toward cell-free serums. But nearly all data are small Phase I/II or preclinical, protocols vary enormously, and **no FDA-approved anti-aging MSC therapy exists (as of 2026).** Injected live-cell therapies carry the highest risk tier here (unregulated clinics, contamination, unknown long-term behavior). The topical "stem-cell serum" versions on shelves contain no living cells and are, at best, growth-factor cosmetics with cosmetic-grade evidence. Promising science, not-ready therapy, dangerous when sold by unregulated clinics — **consult a physician.**
**Stem-cell therapy — Ev 2, Imp 3, Saf 2, Eff 2, Cost 1 → SkinScore 43 (Experimental).**

## Bottom line

**Core winners (75+):** The medical gold standards earn their reputation. **Prescription tretinoin (83)** is the highest-value intervention in this entire cycle — decades of RCTs, direct action on the MMP/collagen lever, cheap, and self-administered. **Botulinum toxin (79)** and **HA fillers (75)** are the two best-evidenced *procedural* options (Cochrane-level and large RCT support), held back only by cost and the need for repeat visits. If you do only three "frontier medical" things with strong evidence behind them, it's these three.

**Best of Promising (55–74):** **Fractional lasers (72)** and **IPL (68)** deliver real, histologically-confirmed remodeling (IPL even nudges the aging gene-expression program). **Microneedling ± RF (70/70)** is the most accessible collagen-induction procedure. Among topicals, **GHK-Cu (71)** and **Matrixyl (66)** are the peptides worth bothering with (safe, plausible, weak-but-nonzero data). **Topical estrogen (63)** is an underused, mechanistically-sound option for menopausal skin, and **topical rapamycin (60)** is the most credible senescence-targeting therapy with actual human RCT markers moving.

**Notable hype that scored low:** **Exosomes (43)** and **stem-cell therapies (43)** are the biggest gap between marketing and evidence — genuinely exciting biology sold years early, unregulated, FDA-unapproved, and at premium prices. **At-home genetic/nutrigenomic skincare tests (40)** are the clearest "skip": near-zero evidence that acting on them improves skin, and their advice collapses into generic sunscreen-and-retinoid guidance anyway. **Senolytic D+Q (50)** shows a real senescence signal but is a systemic chemo-drug regimen, not a skin product. And **"salmon DNA" polynucleotides (59)**, while popular and safe, have their strongest evidence in *wound healing*, not the rejuvenation they're marketed for — the largest trial found no edge over plain hyaluronic acid.

**Cross-cutting honesty:** the injectables and lasers work but don't touch the *biology* of aging — they manage symptoms (muscle pull, volume, pigment). The interventions that actually target Cycle 1's root levers (senescence, SASP, hormonal decline) — rapamycin, senolytics, estrogen, exosomes — are precisely the ones with the thinnest human evidence. That inversion is the defining tension of the frontier. **Everything in this cycle beyond OTC peptides and at-home microneedling requires a licensed dermatologist or physician; treat any clinic selling exosomes, stem cells, or "DNA-personalized" miracles with heavy skepticism.**
