## 1. Skin architecture in 90 seconds

Skin is three layers plus a scaffold:

- **Epidermis** — the outer sheet of keratinocytes that migrate up and flatten into the
  **stratum corneum**, a "brick-and-mortar" barrier of dead cells (bricks) sealed by
  **ceramides, cholesterol and fatty acids** (mortar). This barrier holds water in and irritants
  out. It also holds **melanocytes** (pigment) and immune (Langerhans) cells.
- **Dermal–epidermal junction (DEJ)** — a wavy interface anchoring epidermis to dermis.
  It flattens with age, which weakens nutrient exchange and mechanical grip.
- **Dermis** — the structural core. **Fibroblasts** manufacture **collagen** (mostly type I,
  with type III; the tensile scaffold), **elastin** (recoil), and **glycosaminoglycans** including
  **hyaluronic acid** (water-binding volume). This extracellular matrix (ECM) is what "plumps"
  young skin.
- **Subcutis** — fat and vasculature providing volume, cushioning and blood supply.

Almost every visible sign of aging — wrinkles, sagging, thinning, dullness, uneven tone — traces
back to damage or decline in these components, especially **dermal collagen/elastin** and the
**barrier**.

## 2. Two clocks: intrinsic vs. extrinsic aging

Skin ages on two clocks at once ([Boldpurity summary of intrinsic/extrinsic aging](https://boldpurity.com/blogs/skin-science-journal/how-skin-ages-intrinsic-extrinsic-ageing); [ScienceDirect — Environmentally-Induced (Extrinsic) Skin Aging](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022202X20324088)):

- **Intrinsic (chronological/hormonal) aging** — the unavoidable baseline: fibroblasts slow from
  the mid-20s, cellular repair falters, and hormones (notably estrogen) decline. Produces fine
  lines, thinning, and dryness relatively slowly.
- **Extrinsic aging (the "exposome")** — everything the environment and lifestyle add on top:
  UV, pollution, smoking, diet, heat, stress. Produces the coarse wrinkles, deep sagging, and
  pigment spots most people actually object to.

**The premise of this entire project lives here:** a large majority of *visible* facial aging is
extrinsic, and therefore **modifiable**. Sun exposure alone is commonly cited as responsible for
up to ~80% of visible facial aging — a figure derived mainly from observational and identical-twin
studies, so treat it as directional rather than precise, but the direction is not in doubt. Beyond
"avoid the sun," the exposome gives us many more levers (Section 7).

## 3. The twelve hallmarks of skin aging

The 2024 literature reframes skin aging as **twelve interlocking molecular/cellular mechanisms**,
adapting the canonical "hallmarks of aging" to skin — they act together, not as separate causes of
separate problems ([EvenSkyn — 12 Hallmarks framework](https://www.evenskyn.com/blogs/skin-beautyarticles/hallmarks-of-skin-aging-cellular-framework); [Wyles 2024, *Skin longevity: a paradigm shift in aesthetics*, J Cosmet Dermatol](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jocd.16484)):

1. **Genomic instability** — accumulated DNA damage (UV is a major source).
2. **Telomere attrition** — chromosome-end shortening limiting cell division.
3. **Epigenetic alterations** — drifting gene-expression patterns (the basis of "skin age clocks").
4. **Loss of proteostasis** — misfolded/damaged proteins accumulate.
5. **Impaired macroautophagy** — the cell's recycling system slows.
6. **Deregulated nutrient sensing** — mTOR/insulin/AMPK signaling shifts (why diet and rapamycin-class molecules are of interest).
7. **Mitochondrial dysfunction** — less energy, more reactive oxygen species (ROS).
8. **Cellular senescence** — "zombie" cells that won't divide or die (Section 4.1).
9. **Stem cell exhaustion** — fewer/weaker progenitor cells for renewal.
10. **Altered intercellular communication** — including the SASP and immune signaling.
11. **Chronic inflammation** — "inflammaging" (Section 4.2).
12. **Dysbiosis** — an imbalanced skin (and gut) microbiome.

For intervention purposes, five of these dominate what we can actually influence today. Those get
the deep dive.

## 4. The five drivers that matter most for intervention

### 4.1 Cellular senescence & the SASP
With age and especially **photodamage**, dermal fibroblasts enter **senescence** — a permanent
growth arrest. They stop contributing to repair *and* actively secrete a toxic cocktail, the
**senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP)**: inflammatory cytokines and matrix-degrading
enzymes that break down surrounding collagen and elastin and push neighboring cells toward
senescence too. Accumulated senescent cells are linked to **epidermal thinning, DEJ flattening, and
loss of collagen and elastin** — i.e., the visible aged phenotype ([Hallmarks & Biomarkers of Skin
Senescence, PMC9952625](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9952625/); [Frontiers Physiology
2023 — senescence in skin aging](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2023.1297637/full); [Mechanisms of Senescence and Anti-Senescence Strategies in the Skin, PMC11428750](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11428750/)).
**Implication:** clearing senescent cells (senolytics) or silencing the SASP (senomorphics) is a
frontier lever — see Section 6, scored in Cycle 2.

### 4.2 Inflammaging
**Inflammaging** is chronic, low-grade, symptomless inflammation from a declining ability to
*resolve* inflammation — an imbalance of pro-inflammatory (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β) over
anti-inflammatory signals ([Inflammaging review, PMC12714610](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12714610/); [Cellular Senescence & Inflammaging in the Skin, PMC8068194](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8068194/)).
Senescent cells feed it via the SASP; **UV is one of its most potent accelerators** (ROS + DNA
damage + inflammatory signaling); and an age-shifted **microbiome** and altered skin pH sustain it.
It quietly degrades collagen, weakens the barrier, and slows renewal.
**Implication:** anti-inflammatory levers — diet, actives (niacinamide, retinoids), barrier repair,
UV and pollution defense, sleep/stress — recur across many later cycles.

### 4.3 Glycation & AGEs
Sugars (glucose, fructose) react non-enzymatically with the amino groups of long-lived proteins
like collagen and elastin, forming **advanced glycation end-products (AGEs)** and cross-linking the
fibers. Cross-linked collagen is **stiff, brittle, discolored, and resistant to repair** — visibly,
"sugar sag," lost elasticity, and dullness. In aged skin, the cross-link **glucosepane accounts for
>90%** of collagen cross-links; the process is accelerated by high blood sugar and further driven by
**UV** ([Wang 2024, *Effects of AGEs on skin and anti-glycation strategies*, Exp Dermatol](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/exd.15065); [Danby 2010, *Nutrition and aging skin: sugar and glycation*, PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20620757/); [Frontiers in Medicine 2022 — AGEs in the skin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2022.837222/full)).
Notably, **tight glycemic control over ~4 months reduced glycated-collagen formation by ~25%**, and
high-AGE foods (deep-fried foods, char-grilled/barbecued meats, dark colas) raise systemic AGEs.
**Implication:** glycemic load, cooking methods, and anti-glycation actives are levers for Cycles 4–6.

### 4.4 Oxidative stress, mitochondria & photoaging (the MMP cascade)
UV (and visible light, infrared, and even **heat**) generate **reactive oxygen species** that damage
DNA, lipids and proteins, and — critically — switch on **matrix metalloproteinases (MMP-1, etc.)**,
the enzymes that chew up dermal collagen, while simultaneously **suppressing new collagen synthesis**
by fibroblasts. Aging **mitochondria** leak more ROS, compounding the problem. This ROS→MMP cascade
is the molecular heart of **photoaging** ([The skin aging exposome, J Dermatol Sci — Krutmann et al.](https://www.jdsjournal.com/article/s0923-1811(16)30816-7/fulltext); [Pollution as an exposome factor, Actas Dermo-Sifiliográficas 2025](https://www.actasdermo.org/en-pollution-relevant-exposome-factor-in-avance-S0001731025001000)).
**Implication:** antioxidants (topical/dietary), broad-spectrum photoprotection (including visible
light and infrared/heat), and mitochondrial-support strategies are levers for Cycles 3–6, 8.

### 4.5 Barrier decline, hormones & the microbiome
Three quieter drivers:
- **Barrier:** aging skin makes fewer ceramides and lipids, so it loses water faster and lets more
  irritants in — feeding inflammaging and that "crepey," dull look.
- **Hormones:** estrogen strongly supports fibroblast collagen production; its decline is why skin
  aging accelerates sharply around menopause (Section 5).
- **Microbiome:** age-related shifts in skin flora alter surface pH and lipids, creating a
  permissive environment for chronic low-grade inflammation ([PMC8068194](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8068194/)).
**Implication:** barrier repair, hormone-aware care, and microbiome-friendly routines are levers for
Cycles 4, 6, 7, 8.

## 5. Collagen, by the numbers

Approximate but directionally solid figures ([Art of Skin Care — collagen loss](https://artofskincare.com/blogs/learn/what-causes-collagen-loss); [NCHStats — collagen loss by decade](https://nchstats.com/collagen-loss-by-decade/)):

- Dermal collagen declines roughly **~1% per year from the mid-20s** (intrinsic baseline).
- Around menopause the loss is dramatic: up to **~30% of skin collagen in the first ~5 years**,
  then roughly **~2%/year**, tracking a ~**1.13%/year** fall in skin thickness.
- UV, glycation, smoking, stress-cortisol and poor sleep **add to** this baseline (extrinsic).

Treat the exact percentages as textbook approximations, not precision measurements — but the
pattern (steady decline, hormonal cliff, modifiable extrinsic load) is what matters for planning.

## 6. Frontier callouts (early-stage — flagged, scored in Cycle 2)

- **Senolytics** — drugs that selectively kill senescent cells. The **dasatinib + quercetin (D+Q)**
  combination has shown it *can* clear senescent human cells; skin-specific, durable cosmetic benefit
  in humans is **not yet established** ([Senescence as a molecular target, PubMed 39929368](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39929368/); [Leveraging Senotherapeutics, PMC10873061](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10873061/)).
- **Senomorphics** — silence the SASP without killing cells. **Topical rapamycin** (an mTOR
  inhibitor) reduced wrinkles and sagging, increased dermal volume, and lowered the senescence
  marker p16 in early human work — promising but small ([PMC11428750](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11428750/)).
- Also on the Cycle 2 docket: exosomes, growth factors, peptides, microneedling/PRP, energy devices,
  and nutrigenomics.

These are genuinely exciting and genuinely unproven for everyday cosmetic use. SkinScore will grade
them honestly — most will land in **Experimental** until human evidence catches up.

## 8. What's already solid vs. mostly hype

- **Solid, high-confidence today:** broad-spectrum photoprotection; not smoking; the reality of the
  ROS→MMP photoaging cascade; senescence and inflammaging as real drivers; glycation stiffening
  collagen. These will anchor the protocol.
- **Real but oversold:** dietary "collagen" as a cure-all; most "collagen-boosting" cosmetic claims;
  antioxidant serums (some work, many don't at real-world doses) — all to be scored, not assumed.
- **Frontier, unproven for daily cosmetic use:** senolytics, exosomes, most at-home energy devices —
  exciting, but Experimental until the human data lands.
