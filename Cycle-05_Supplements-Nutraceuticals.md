# Cycle 5 — Supplements & Nutraceuticals

*Educational synthesis, not medical advice. Supplements are the most marketing-saturated corner of skin health, and the gap between what a label promises and what a placebo-controlled trial shows is often enormous. This cycle grades oral interventions against the [Cycle 1](./Cycle-01_Foundational-Levers.md) levers — **oxidative stress, inflammaging, photoaging/photoprotection, glycation, barrier, and microbiome** — and applies one discipline throughout: separating "corrects a deficiency" (where a nutrient can produce real benefit in a depleted person) from "benefits an already-replete person" (where most of the money is spent and most of the evidence evaporates). Where trials are industry-funded, small, or heterogeneous, that is stated plainly, and supplement–drug interactions and upper-limit/safety issues are flagged for professional guidance. The single most important safety message: a "natural" pill is still a pharmacologically active dose, and a few of these carry genuine harm.*

---

## The deficiency-vs-replete axis (read this first)

Almost every finding below collapses onto one question: **were the subjects depleted?** Vitamin D, zinc, and vitamin C behave like drugs in a deficient person and like inert filler in a replete one. Photoprotective and structural agents (nicotinamide, carotenoids, collagen) act on a *pathway* rather than a deficiency, so they can help replete people — but the effect sizes are modest and the trials are frequently funded by the ingredient's manufacturer. Treat any "clinically proven" claim as a prompt to ask: *proven by whom, at what dose, in whom, versus placebo, and did an independent lab replicate it?*

## Photoprotection & skin-cancer prevention (photoaging lever)

**Nicotinamide (niacinamide), oral — the strongest evidence in this cycle.** The [ONTRAC phase 3 RCT](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1506197) (n=386 immunocompetent adults with ≥2 prior keratinocyte cancers, 500 mg twice daily for 12 months) cut new non-melanoma skin cancers by **23%** and actinic keratoses by ~13–20% versus placebo, with no meaningful toxicity. Mechanism ties directly to Cycle 1: nicotinamide replenishes NAD+, preventing UV-induced ATP depletion and **enhancing DNA repair** while blunting UV immunosuppression. A [systematic review/meta-analysis](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/1/100) supports the chemopreventive signal. Crucial honesty: the benefit does **not** generalise to everyone. The [ONTRANS trial](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMc2303841) in immunosuppressed solid-organ transplant recipients found **no reduction** in keratinocyte cancers or AKs, and a [2025 cohort analysis](https://www.dermatologytimes.com/view/oral-nicotinamide-not-beneficial-in-preventing-skin-cancer-in-organ-transplant-recipients-) confirmed no overall benefit in that group. So nicotinamide is a targeted tool for **high-risk immunocompetent** people, not a general anti-ageing pill (crucially, do not confuse nicotinamide with **niacin/nicotinic acid**, which causes flushing and hepatotoxicity — the two are chemically distinct). Safety is otherwise excellent per a [JAAD safety review](https://www.jaadreviews.org/article/S2950-1989(25)00079-0/fulltext).

**Oral carotenoids (β-carotene, lycopene, mixed) — real but modest systemic SPF.** A [meta-analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0002916523030265) found β-carotene provides significant protection against UV-induced erythema **only when taken ≥10 weeks**, dose-dependently (≥12 mg/day). [Lycopene/lutein crossover data](https://academic.oup.com/bjd/article/176/5/1231/6661085) and [mixed-carotenoid RCTs](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/phpp.12541) show reduced UVB erythema and UVA pigmentation; tomato-derived lycopene outperformed synthetic. This raises the minimal erythema dose modestly — roughly the equivalent of a low single-digit SPF — and is an **adjunct, never a sunscreen replacement**. **Major safety flag:** the [ATBC](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29889248/) and CARET trials found synthetic β-carotene (20–30 mg/day) **increased lung cancer incidence ~18% and mortality in current smokers and asbestos-exposed workers**. Anyone who smokes should avoid β-carotene supplements entirely; lycopene/lutein carry no such signal.

**Polypodium leucotomos extract (PLE / Fernblock) — plausible adjunct, thin data.** A [JAAD systematic review](https://www.jaad.org/article/S0190-9622(19)31153-3/pdf) of 18 studies (samples n=5–61) reported increased MED and reduced UV erythema; a [recent MED evaluation](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00403-025-04055-8) reinforced this at 240 mg twice daily. Mechanism hits the oxidative-stress and photoaging levers (scavenges ROS, reduces UV-induced DNA/matrix damage). Reality check: studies are small, short, and mostly on one proprietary formulation. Useful for melasma or photosensitivity patients as an add-on; not a standalone.

## Structural / anti-ageing supplements (glycation, barrier, inflammaging)

**Hydrolyzed collagen peptides — popular, positive on paper, but the funding shadow is huge.** The largest synthesis, a [2025 meta-analysis of 23 RCTs (n=1,474)](https://www.amjmed.com/article/S0002-9343(25)00283-9/abstract), found significant improvements in hydration, elasticity, and wrinkles — *but* studies **without** industry funding showed **no effect**, and **high-quality studies showed no significant effect in any category** while only low-quality studies showed benefit. That is a textbook funding/quality-bias signature and the most important sentence in this cycle. A separate [systematic review](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41430-026-01778-3) found benefit in most trials but rated the majority at **high risk of bias**. Dose-response work ([Proksch/VERISOL 2.5 g](https://www.jarlife.net/2889-specific-natural-bioactive-type-1-collagen-peptides-oral-intake-reverse-skin-aging-signs-in-mature-women.html); pooled RCTs) suggests 2.5–10 g/day with no added benefit above 10 g. Mechanism is real in principle — digested peptides (Pro-Hyp, Hyp-Gly) may signal fibroblasts — but the honest verdict is **"probably a small real effect, systematically inflated by industry trials."** Safe and easy; manage expectations.

**Oral hyaluronic acid — small, mostly manufacturer-run trials.** A cluster of 12-week RCTs (e.g. [120 mg/day sodium hyaluronate](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-32758-5), [JDD efficacy trial](https://jddonline.com/articles/oral-hyaluronic-acid-supplement-efficacy-in-skin-hydration-elasticity-wrinkle-depth-reduction-S1545961625P8542X/), [12-week hydration RCT](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8308347/)) report improved hydration and reduced wrinkle depth. Only ~7 RCTs exist total, several ingredient-funded, and the mechanism (ingested HA is degraded to fragments/monosaccharides, not delivered intact to dermis) is biologically murky. Very safe; evidence is preliminary and commercially conflicted.

**Oral vitamin C — a cofactor, not a standalone hero.** Vitamin C is an essential cofactor for prolyl/lysyl hydroxylase in **collagen synthesis** and a systemic antioxidant, per a [dermatology systematic review](https://jcadonline.com/clinical-applications-of-vitamin-c-in-dermatology-a-systematic-review/). But standalone oral vitamin C for cosmetic skin outcomes has **weak** independent evidence; most positive signals come from **combination** formulas (with collagen, carotenoids, vitamin E) or from correcting genuine deficiency (where its role is real — think scorbutic skin). It genuinely matters if you are depleted; for a replete person eating produce, an isolated pill adds little. Cheap and safe (GI upset >1 g).

**Astaxanthin — promising small RCTs, unconvinced systematic reviews.** Individual RCTs at 4–6 mg/day show improved elasticity, reduced wrinkles/TEWL, and increased MED ([UV-deterioration RCT](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6073124/)), acting on the oxidative-stress and photoaging levers as a potent carotenoid antioxidant. But a [2025 systematic review/meta-analysis](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12318760/) concluded there is **insufficient evidence** to recommend it for photoaging, while confirming it is safe. Genuinely promising, not yet proven.

**Resveratrol — great biology, poor bioavailability.** Small RCTs (e.g. an [8-week trial in women >40](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12757695/)) report reduced visible ageing and Nrf2/antioxidant-pathway activation. The persistent problem across the literature is **very low oral bioavailability** — most is metabolised before reaching skin — so clinical relevance is uncertain and effects may actually be driven by gut-microbiome metabolites. Safe to ~1 g; evidence remains experimental.

**Oral ceramides / phytoceramides — the barrier play, and the most credible "structural" supplement.** A [meta-analysis of 11 studies (n=601, 6–20 weeks)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9201759/) found a **statistically significant increase in hydration and decrease in TEWL** — directly hitting the barrier lever. Wheat-derived glucosylceramides (~350 mg/day) and rice ceramides (~40 mg/day) have the best track records; a [milk-ceramide RCT](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1756464624000100) also showed hydration/wrinkle benefit. Effects are on hydration/barrier (not dramatic wrinkle reversal), take 4–8 weeks, and wheat-derived products are unsuitable for those avoiding gluten.

## Deficiency-correction & inflammatory-skin supplements

**Vitamin D — valuable when you are deficient, largely inert when you are not.** For atopic dermatitis, a [systematic review/meta-analysis (11 RCTs, n=686)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11644640/) and adjuvant RCTs ([1,600 IU/day improved EASI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7609811/); [winter AD in children](https://www.jacionline.org/article/S0091-6749(14)01114-2/fulltext)) show benefit — concentrated in **deficient/winter** populations, plausibly via cathelicidin/barrier and immune modulation (inflammaging + barrier levers). For psoriasis, benefit appears mainly in those with [lower baseline 25(OH)D](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10061314/). It is **not** a cosmetic anti-wrinkle supplement. **Safety flag:** fat-soluble and cumulative — chronic high doses cause hypercalcemia/toxicity; correct a measured deficiency rather than megadosing blindly, and involve a clinician.

**Zinc — legitimate for acne, best as adjunct, watch the dose.** A [systematic review/meta-analysis](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/dth.14252) found acne patients have significantly **lower serum zinc**, and zinc supplementation significantly reduced inflammatory papule counts as monotherapy or adjunct, with no excess adverse events at studied doses. Hits the inflammaging lever. **Safety flags:** doses >40 mg/day elemental can cause nausea and, chronically, **copper deficiency**; zinc also **interferes with absorption of some antibiotics** (tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones) — separate dosing and consult a pharmacist.

**Omega-3 (EPA/DHA fish oil) — biologically sound, clinically mixed.** A [review of cutaneous inflammatory disorders](https://jintegrativederm.org/doi/10.64550/joid.dc39qy52) found **mixed efficacy** across acne, AD, psoriasis, and rosacea. Signals exist: an acne RCT (2 g/day) reduced inflammatory and non-inflammatory lesions; [AD trials](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11397185/) show symptom/QoL improvement; a [psoriasis meta-analysis](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6896351/) is modestly favourable. Mechanism is squarely anti-inflammatory (resolvins, reduced arachidonic-acid cascade) → inflammaging lever. **Safety flags:** the [EFSA](https://www.boltpharmacy.co.uk/guide/can-you-take-too-much-omega-3) considers ≤5 g/day EPA+DHA tolerable and the FDA calls ≤3 g/day GRAS; a [meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11179820/) found no clinically significant bleeding increase, but caution and professional guidance are warranted with **anticoagulants/antiplatelets** and before surgery. Avoid cod-liver oil for skin dosing (vitamin A/D toxicity risk).

**Oral probiotics — a microbiome-lever idea outrunning its data.** A [2025 systematic review/meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12734792/) of 3 acne RCTs (n=231) found a modest reduction in inflammatory lesions (SMD −0.57) but **substantial heterogeneity (I²=72%)** and few trials. AD reviews show SCORAD reductions with various *Lactobacillus/Bifidobacterium* strains, but strains, doses, and endpoints are inconsistent. Directly targets the gut–skin/microbiome lever conceptually; the human evidence is early, strain-specific, and not yet actionable as a general recommendation.

**Biotin — the myth-check. For skin/hair in a replete person: skip, and it can actively harm your lab results.** Multiple reviews ([JCAD](https://jcadonline.com/biotin-for-hair-loss-evidence/); [skintherapyletter](https://www.skintherapyletter.com/dermatology/skin-hair-nail-supplements-evidence/)) conclude there is **no good evidence** biotin improves hair, skin, or nails in people **without** a deficiency — and true biotin deficiency is rare. It genuinely helps only defined states (biotinidase deficiency, uncombable-hair syndrome, malabsorption, valproate/isotretinoin use). **Real harm flag:** high-dose biotin causes clinically significant **interference with immunoassays** — falsely distorting thyroid, cardiac troponin, and hormone tests, which has led to misdiagnosis ([JAAD editorial "first do no harm"](https://www.jaad.org/article/S0190-9622(24)00576-0/fulltext)). Tell any lab/physician if you take it. This is the clearest "marketing myth" in the cycle.

## Bottom line

- **The one supplement with genuinely strong, independent RCT evidence is oral nicotinamide** — but only for **skin-cancer/AK prevention in high-risk immunocompetent people** (ONTRAC), and it **failed** to replicate in transplant recipients. It is not an anti-wrinkle pill.
- **Deficiency correction is where oral nutrients earn their keep.** Vitamin D (in deficient AD/psoriasis patients), zinc (acne), and vitamin C (if depleted) can do real work. In a replete, well-fed person, most of that benefit disappears — a fact the supplement industry is not motivated to advertise.
- **Collagen peptides are the cautionary tale of the category:** a large, seemingly positive RCT base whose benefit **vanishes in non-industry-funded and high-quality trials.** Probably a small real effect, heavily inflated by conflicted studies. Safe and cheap, so reasonable to try — but calibrate expectations to "modest," not "transformative."
- **Carotenoids and PLE add a low-single-digit systemic photoprotection** that is a genuine *adjunct* to — never a substitute for — topical sunscreen (Cycle 3 territory).
- **Ceramides/phytoceramides** are the most credible barrier-support supplement (hydration/TEWL). **Astaxanthin, resveratrol, HA, and probiotics** are promising-but-unproven — worth watching, not worth strong claims.
- **Biotin for skin in a replete person is a marketing myth** — and worse, high doses **corrupt common blood tests** (thyroid, troponin), a real clinical harm. Skip it unless you have a diagnosed deficiency, and always disclose it before lab work.
- **Safety, flagged for professional guidance:** smokers must avoid **β-carotene** (lung-cancer risk, ATBC/CARET); **vitamin D** and **cod-liver oil** are fat-soluble and toxic in excess; **omega-3** warrants caution with anticoagulants/antiplatelets and before surgery; **zinc** blocks tetracycline/fluoroquinolone absorption and depletes copper long-term; **biotin** distorts immunoassays. None of this is medical advice — discuss additions with a physician or pharmacist, especially alongside prescription drugs.
